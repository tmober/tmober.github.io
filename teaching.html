Teaching

Failure is instructive. The person who really thinks learns quite as much from [...] failures as from [...] successes. — John Dewey

Given that my research is related to teaching and learning, I feel fortunate that my research area is aligned with my teaching. As a result, I have had opportunities to be actively involved in organizations that aim to support the preparation of new instructors for college teaching. From July 2017 to December 2018, I served as the Chair of the Graduate Student Teaching Association (GSTA), an entity of the Society for the Teaching of Psychology (STP; Division 2 of the American Psychological Association). Through this work, I helped organize pedagogy-themed conferences, workshops, edited the GSTA blog on the STP website, contributed to a 2017 handbook on the teaching of psychology, and edited a handbook on teaching psychology aimed at graduate student and first-time instructors, How We Teach Now: The GSTA Guide to Transformative Teaching published in 2020. In the summer of both 2018 and 2019, I also served as a peer mentor for the Teach@CUNY Summer Institute, which consisted of a week-long series of seminars and workshops designed to facilitate a dialogue around best practices for college teaching, and to prepare graduate students who were assigned to serve as instructors or teaching assistants for the first time. These opportunities have provided a chance to reflect deeply on my teaching as well. In the process, I have learned a lot about psychological theories and research, as well as its history and epistemology. I have also learned a lot about myself and other people. In the years to come, I hope to continue teaching psychology, particularly in the areas of cognitive, developmental, and educational psychology, as well as research methods and statistics, and in so doing, to further improve my practice of teaching while preparing future generations of potential scholars and teachers.

Cognitive Processes | Hunter College, CUNY | PSYCH 243
The purpose of this course is to offer a survey of theoretical issues and empirical research on human cognition including attention, perception, memory, representation of knowledge, language, and problem solving. It aims to present students with an introduction to the field of cognitive psychology including a historical view of cognitive psychology, the neural basis for cognition, perception, memory, imagery, general knowledge and language. This course explores new discoveries about how we think and how we investigate thinking.

Graduate Courses

Research and Evaluation Methods in Behavioral Science | New York University | APSY GE2070
This course aims to offer the skills to analyze and evaluate research studies in the behavioral sciences. There is an emphasis on the science of research, developing hypotheses, sampling, defining and measuring variables, types of experimental and non-experimental designs, qualitative designs, evaluation designs, and ethics in research. Students gain experience designing and critiquing their own studies.

Courses Served as Teaching Assistant

Introductory Statistics for the Health Professions | New York University | GN 2005
This intensive course is intended for consumers of statistics in the biological, public health, nursing, and medical fields. It will concentrate on the interpretation and comprehension of graphical and statistical techniques that are important components of the medical, nursing, and public health literature. Presentation of statistical material will be linked to its application as reported in the nursing and medical literature. Although some mathematics will be presented, it is not expected that the student will memorize formulas. The course will present some basic mathematical calculations, but regular calculations are not required. Students are encouraged to follow the calculations in the lecture notes and the articles assigned in the course. It is recommended that students use a calculator (that has square and square root functions) to follow some of the basic calculations presented.
Experimental Psychology: General | Hunter College, CUNY | PSYCH 250
This course provides an introduction to the basic methods used for conducting human psychological research with an emphasis on research design and identifying the basic components of a research report. The lecture portion of the course aims to provide a conceptual foundation for understanding how to plan, conduct, analyze, and evaluate experiments, includes consideration of common problems encountered when designing studies, techniques that can be used to surmount these problems, and illustrates effective and ineffective designs with concrete examples. These examples will be drawn from primary research articles that will be discussed in the lecture and are posted on Blackboard.The laboratory portion of the course provides a more practical, hands-on approach, as students apply the basic concepts covered in lecture to the conduct of experiments. Students will experience experimentation from the perspectives of experimenter and participant. The main focus of the lab is to provide a chance for students to design and carry out experiments during the course of the semester in collaborative groups. Reports written in APA style will be completed for all four of the experiments, and in addition students will give an individual oral presentation of the results of their fourth experiment.
