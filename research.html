Research

As a current Postdoctoral Research Associate in the Learning Analytics and Measurement in Behavioral Sciences (LAMBS) Lab at the University of Notre Dame. The LAMBS lab is particularly concerned with measurement issues in psychological assessment and educational testing, with a particular focus on the applications of item response theory (IRT). My present and intended future research broadly overlaps with educational psychology and the learning sciences, as well as developmental and cognitive educational psychology. In the course of pursuing my doctoral degree and in the years since serving as a postdoctoral researcher, I have developed a range of analytic and computational skills.

Interdisciplinary collaborations have proven themselves to be quite productive and I intend to continue seeking out such collaborations. I have prior experience working with an interdisciplinary design and research team that develops and studies game-based cognitive skills interventions at the CREATE Lab of New York University. I have presented this line of work at national conferences and have been a co-author on publications describing this work which have appeared in Mind, Brain, & Education, Computers & Education, Learning & Instruction, and Cognitive Development. I have contributed to projects that examine the trade-offs of different design elements, including exploring parameters that induce more challenge within the game, adjusting aspects of the game that make it more emotionally salient, and developing adaptive features that adjust to the player’s performance. Through this line of work, I have developed an interest in studying engagement within the context of these games.

Prior to my current line of research, my dissertation work was motivated by an interest in understanding the associations between decoding, a foundational literacy skill, and executive functions, which describe a group of top-down, domain-general cognitive skills. In an effort to synthesize the research that has been done on this topic, I conducted a meta-analysis to determine an overall average correlation between these two constructs and to examine factors that could mediate the association. Findings from this line of work now appear in Educational Psychology Review. Leading up to my current dissertation research, I conducted research examining associations between executive functions and literacy among a sample of adolescent students. Work from this project has been presented at national and international conferences, and was recently published in Reading Psychology. I conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of the associations between executive functions and the requisite literacy skill of decoding.

Research Interests

Some current research areas include the following:

  • Measuring engagement and learning within applied educational contexts
  • Using educational technologies to promote engagement and learning
  • Executive functions and reading
  • Improving pedagogical rigor through the scholarship of teaching and learning
